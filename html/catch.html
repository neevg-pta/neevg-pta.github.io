Carolina Adapts Toys for Children
CATCH is a student-run organization at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill that "adapts" the circuitry of electronic toys for children with special needs. Toys are donated to hospitals and rehabilitation centers and any student is welcome to come and help!
Putting Together My First Toys
My first CATCH meeting was at an event called "Late Night with Toys." This semesterly event brings together UNC students as a part of a four hours toy-adapting marathon.
After going through the orientation session, I partnered up and used a guide to configure my first toy. Over the next four hours, I worked with my partner to follow the guide to rewire a toy fire truck and redo some of our mistakes along the way. By the end of the four hours, I was exhausted -- but I was glad to see that I had finished rewiring my first toy.
Over the next few months, I attended weekly Tuesday meetings to continue learning how to take apart toys and rewire them for children with disabilities. Eventually, the whole process became second hand nature -- I would have to even reference the guide to attach and external button on a mew toy. I was able to start helping other groups troubleshoot their problems and understood how the wiring of the toys worked. Since then, I have finished and troubleshooted over 10 toys!
Team Lead Training
Halfway through the school year, CATCH announced a new position -- Team Lead. Team Leads would need to devote additional time each week to undergo quality assurance training. Then, Team Leads would leads groups during regular meetings to ensure that the toys we were making as a club were achieving quality standards.
We were instructed by a profesisonal with over 20 years of experience in soldering professionally. He taught us how to properly apply flux and solder to wire joints so that the conneciton was secure and would not be prone to breaking.
Thrift Toys
Near the end of the year, CATCH received a donation of toys from a thrift store. They asked for volunteers to take apart these toys, figure out how they worked, and then configure them for children with disabilities. Looking for a challenge, I volunteered to take on this task.
At first, I had trouble recognizing what wires I would have to cut and reconfigure in order to attach an external button. With the help of a mentor, I planned out what changes to make. Even though it took me longer to finish than the standard set of toys, I was happy that I had overcome the challenges I faced along the way.
